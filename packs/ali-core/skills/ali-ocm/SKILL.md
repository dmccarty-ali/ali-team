# Organizational Change Management

## When I Speak Up

This skill activates when the conversation involves:

**Planning/Design:**
- Selecting an OCM framework (ADKAR, Kotter, Prosci, Lewin)
- Designing stakeholder engagement strategies
- Planning change impact assessments across process, tech, role, mindset dimensions
- Architecting communication plans with audience segmentation
- Designing training programs and data literacy curricula
- Planning readiness assessments and go/no-go criteria
- Building resistance management strategies

**Implementation:**
- Writing communication plans with audience-specific messaging
- Building stakeholder analysis matrices and power/interest grids
- Creating change impact matrices with severity ratings
- Developing train-the-trainer programs
- Designing adoption metric frameworks
- Building change champion networks and activation plans
- Writing readiness survey instruments
- Designing data literacy programs by persona

**Guidance/Best Practices:**
- Asking when to use ADKAR vs Kotter vs Prosci vs Lewin
- Asking how to structure a stakeholder analysis
- Asking how to handle resistance without derailing timelines
- Asking what metrics prove adoption success
- Asking how to build WIIFM messaging for executives vs end users
- Asking how to sustain adoption post go-live

**Review/Validation:**
- Reviewing OCM deliverables for completeness
- Auditing stakeholder coverage (any groups missed?)
- Validating communication channel selection
- Checking training design against Bloom's taxonomy
- Assessing readiness criteria rigor
- Evaluating adoption metric balance (leading vs lagging)

## Key Principles

- **Framework fit matters**: ADKAR for individual focus, Kotter for org-wide transformation, Prosci for structured methodology, Lewin for simple transitions - match to context
- **Stakeholder segmentation is foundational**: Different groups need different messaging, timing, and support
- **Impact has four dimensions**: Process change, technology change, role change, mindset/culture change - assess all four
- **WIIFM drives adoption**: "What's In It For Me" is the primary motivator - lead with benefits, not features
- **Resistance is information**: Resistance signals unaddressed concerns - diagnose root cause before responding
- **Readiness gates protect launch**: Go/no-go criteria prevent launching into failure
- **Metrics must be actionable**: Track leading indicators (training completion, system logins) to intervene before lagging indicators (error rates, non-compliance) solidify
- **Sustainability requires embedding**: Build change into governance, roles, and processes - not just a launch event
- **Champion networks scale reach**: Peer-to-peer influence is more effective than top-down mandates alone
- **Data literacy is a journey**: Maturity levels require different interventions - don't treat executives and analysts the same

## 1. Change Management Frameworks

### Framework Selection Decision Matrix

| Scenario | Recommended Framework | Why |
|----------|-----------------------|-----|
| Individual behavior change is the bottleneck | ADKAR (Prosci) | Targets individual awareness, desire, knowledge, ability, reinforcement |
| Large-scale org transformation with exec mandate | Kotter's 8 Steps | Creates urgency, coalition, and structural embedding |
| Structured project with defined methodology | Prosci 3-Phase | Provides repeatable process: Prepare, Manage, Reinforce |
| Simple, bounded change with clear before/after | Lewin's Unfreeze/Change/Refreeze | Lightweight, easy to communicate |
| Cultural or mindset change required | Kotter + ADKAR hybrid | Kotter handles org level; ADKAR handles individual level |
| Technology adoption (ERP, data platform) | ADKAR or Prosci | Individual adoption is the critical path |
| Regulatory/compliance-driven change | Prosci 3-Phase | Documentation and audit trail built in |

### ADKAR Model

**Goal:** Address individual transitions - people change one person at a time.

| Element | Definition | Interventions |
|---------|------------|---------------|
| **A - Awareness** | Why change is needed | Town halls, leadership messaging, FAQ documents |
| **D - Desire** | Motivation to support the change | WIIFM messaging, manager conversations, success stories |
| **K - Knowledge** | How to change | Training, job aids, simulations, shadowing |
| **A - Ability** | Demonstrated new behavior | Practice, coaching, on-the-job support |
| **R - Reinforcement** | Sustain the change | Recognition, incentives, accountability, metrics |

**Barrier point principle:** The weakest element blocks progress. Diagnose before intervening.

### Kotter's 8 Steps

| Step | Phase | Key Activity |
|------|-------|--------------|
| 1. Create urgency | Create climate | Communicate burning platform; what happens if we don't change |
| 2. Form guiding coalition | Create climate | Build cross-functional leadership team with credibility |
| 3. Create vision and strategy | Create climate | Articulate clear, inspiring destination |
| 4. Communicate the vision | Engage organization | Consistent messaging across all channels |
| 5. Empower broad-based action | Engage organization | Remove barriers; enable experimentation |
| 6. Generate short-term wins | Engage organization | Celebrate early successes visibly |
| 7. Consolidate gains | Implement and sustain | Build on wins; accelerate |
| 8. Anchor new approaches | Implement and sustain | Embed in culture, hiring, and processes |

### Prosci 3-Phase Process

| Phase | Focus | Key Deliverables |
|-------|-------|-----------------|
| **Phase 1: Prepare Approach** | Define change; build strategy | Change definition, OCM strategy, sponsorship model |
| **Phase 2: Manage Change** | Execute plans | Sponsor roadmap, stakeholder engagement plan, communication plan, training plan |
| **Phase 3: Reinforce Outcomes** | Sustain adoption | Adoption measurement, corrective actions, after-action review |

### Lewin's Change Model

```
UNFREEZE → CHANGE → REFREEZE

Unfreeze: Destabilize status quo. Create awareness that change is needed.
          Reduce forces resisting change. Increase forces driving change.

Change:   Transition period. Support through uncertainty.
          Provide knowledge, coaching, quick wins.

Refreeze: Stabilize new state. Embed in processes, policies, culture.
          Celebrate success. Recognize new behaviors.
```

**When Lewin is insufficient:** Lewin assumes a clear end state. For complex, continuous change use ADKAR or Prosci.

## 2. Stakeholder Analysis

### Stakeholder Identification Categories

| Category | Description | OCM Implication |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Sponsors** | Executives with authority and accountability | Must be active and visible; coach sponsors on their role |
| **Change Targets** | People whose work changes | Primary adoption focus; assess impact by group |
| **Advocates / Champions** | Willing early adopters | Recruit as change champions; amplify messaging |
| **Resistors** | Opposed to change | Diagnose root cause; targeted engagement |
| **Influencers** | Informal authority; others follow them | Engage early; convert to advocates if possible |
| **Bystanders** | Aware but disengaged | Risk of passive non-adoption; ensure awareness |

### Power/Interest Grid

```
HIGH POWER
    │
    │  KEEP SATISFIED      │  MANAGE CLOSELY
    │  (high power,        │  (high power,
    │   low interest)      │   high interest)
    │                      │
    ├──────────────────────┼──────────────────────
    │                      │
    │  MONITOR             │  KEEP INFORMED
    │  (low power,         │  (low power,
    │   low interest)      │   high interest)
    │                      │
LOW POWER
    └──────────────────────────────────────────────
              LOW INTEREST        HIGH INTEREST
```

**Strategies by quadrant:**

| Quadrant | Strategy | Frequency |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| Manage Closely | Deep engagement, co-creation, frequent updates | Weekly |
| Keep Satisfied | Status briefings, escalation path | Bi-weekly |
| Keep Informed | Newsletters, all-hands, FAQ | Monthly |
| Monitor | General communications | As needed |

### Stakeholder Analysis Template

| Stakeholder Group | Role | Impact Level (H/M/L) | Current Attitude (-2 to +2) | Target Attitude | Key Concerns | Engagement Strategy | Owner |
|-------------------|------|---------------------|------------------------------|-----------------|--------------|--------------------|----|
| Executive Sponsors | Sponsor | H | +1 | +2 | ROI, timeline | Monthly executive briefing | CoS |
| Department Managers | Target | H | 0 | +1 | Staff workload, process disruption | Change champion program | OCM Lead |
| End Users | Target | H | -1 | +1 | Job security, skill gaps | Training + peer support | Training Lead |
| IT Team | Enabler | M | +1 | +2 | Technical debt, support burden | Technical working group | IT Lead |

**Attitude scale:** -2 (active resistor), -1 (passive resistor), 0 (neutral), +1 (supporter), +2 (active advocate)

### Influence Mapping

For complex stakeholder landscapes, map influence relationships:

```
[Executive Sponsor] ──influences──> [Dept Head A]
[Dept Head A] ──manages──> [Team Lead 1] ──peers──> [Team Lead 2]
[Informal Leader X] ──influences──> [15 end users]
```

**Strategy:** Identify the informal leaders. Converting one influential peer impacts many.

### RACI for Change Activities

| Activity | Executive Sponsor | OCM Lead | Dept Managers | End Users | IT |
|----------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Kick-off town hall | A | R | C | I | I |
| Stakeholder interviews | I | A/R | C | C | C |
| Communication plan approval | A | R | C | - | I |
| Training delivery | I | A | C | R | I |
| Go/no-go decision | A | C | C | I | C |
| Post-launch reinforcement | A | R | R | C | C |

**R** = Responsible, **A** = Accountable, **C** = Consulted, **I** = Informed

### Stakeholder Interview Guide

Use for change impact discovery and resistance early detection:

1. What does your team do today related to [process/system]?
2. What will change for your team specifically?
3. What concerns do you have about this change?
4. Who else in your organization should we be talking to?
5. What would make this change successful from your perspective?
6. What would cause this to fail?
7. Who are the informal leaders your team listens to?
8. What support would your team need?

## 3. Change Impact Assessment

### Four Dimensions of Change Impact

| Dimension | What Changes | Examples |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Process** | How work gets done; steps, approvals, handoffs | New data entry workflow, different approval chain, eliminated manual steps |
| **Technology** | Tools, systems, interfaces used | New data platform, different reporting tool, new governance portal |
| **Role** | Responsibilities, skills required, job structure | New data steward role, analyst now accountable for data quality, manager approval required |
| **Mindset/Culture** | Beliefs, norms, values | Data is an asset not a cost, trust the system not the spreadsheet, share data across silos |

### Impact Severity Rating

| Rating | Definition | OCM Response |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| **High (3)** | Fundamentally changes how this group works | Intensive engagement, dedicated training, ongoing coaching |
| **Medium (2)** | Moderate changes requiring adaptation | Standard training, manager coaching, communication |
| **Low (1)** | Minor changes, mostly familiar | General communications, FAQ, awareness |
| **None (0)** | No meaningful impact | General awareness only |

### Change Saturation Analysis

Assess competing initiatives to identify overloaded groups:

| Stakeholder Group | Initiative A | Initiative B | Initiative C | Total Load | Risk Level |
|-------------------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Finance Team | H | M | - | 5 | High |
| IT Operations | H | H | L | 7 | Critical |
| End Users (Sales) | L | - | H | 4 | Medium |
| Executives | M | M | M | 6 | High |

**Scoring:** High=3, Medium=2, Low=1, None=0. Total >5 = high risk for change fatigue.

**Mitigation options:** Stagger launches, reduce scope, prioritize sequencing, increase support resources.

### Change Impact Matrix Template

| Stakeholder Group | Process Impact | Technology Impact | Role Impact | Mindset Impact | Overall Severity | Priority Actions |
|-------------------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|------|
| Data Stewards | H | H | H | M | Critical | Dedicated role training, RACI clarity, peer support group |
| Business Analysts | M | H | M | H | High | Platform training, data trust messaging, Q&A sessions |
| Department Managers | L | L | M | H | Medium | Manager toolkit, WIIFM briefing, approval workflow guide |
| Executives | L | L | L | M | Low | Executive dashboard, quarterly briefing |

## 4. Communication Strategy

### Why/What/How/WIIFM Framework

Every communication to every audience must answer these four questions:

| Question | Purpose | Example |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Why** | Burning platform; reason for change | "Our data is siloed in 12 systems. We cannot answer basic questions without 2 weeks of manual work." |
| **What** | What is changing and what is not | "We are implementing Collibra as a single data catalog. Your existing tools remain; this adds visibility." |
| **How** | Process and timeline | "Pilot starts March 1. Your team goes live in Phase 2 (June). Training starts May 15." |
| **WIIFM** | Personal benefit | "You will spend 3 fewer hours per week hunting for data definitions. Your reports will be trusted." |

### Audience Segmentation by Message Type

| Audience | Why Emphasis | What Emphasis | How Emphasis | WIIFM Emphasis |
|----------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| C-Suite | High (ROI, risk) | Medium | Low | High (strategic outcomes) |
| Department Managers | High (team impact) | High | High (timeline) | High (operational relief) |
| End Users | Medium | High (what exactly changes) | High (step-by-step) | Very High (daily job impact) |
| IT | Medium | High (technical scope) | Very High | High (reduced firefighting) |
| Data Stewards | Medium | High (new responsibilities) | Very High | High (role clarity, authority) |

### Channel Selection Matrix

| Channel | Best For | Cadence | Reach |
|---------|----------|---------|-------|
| Executive town hall | Burning platform, sponsorship visibility | Launch + quarterly | All staff |
| Manager cascade | WIIFM by team, local context | Pre-launch + monthly | Dept-specific |
| Email newsletter | Status updates, milestones, wins | Bi-weekly | Broad |
| Intranet / SharePoint | Reference materials, FAQ, training links | Updated continuously | Self-service |
| Team meetings (manager-led) | Local concerns, Q&A, resistance surface | Monthly | Small groups |
| Training sessions | Knowledge and ability building | Per training calendar | Role-specific |
| Champion office hours | Peer support, hands-on help | Weekly post-launch | Opt-in |
| Video (async) | Process walkthroughs, exec messages | Key milestones | Broad |
| Slack / Teams channels | Real-time Q&A, quick wins | Ongoing | Self-selected |

### Communication Cadence

| Phase | Timing | Key Messages | Channels |
|-------|--------|--------------|---------|
| **Awareness** | 8-12 weeks pre-launch | Why change, what is coming | Town hall, email, manager cascade |
| **Preparation** | 4-8 weeks pre-launch | Training schedule, what to expect | Email, intranet, team meetings |
| **Launch** | Launch week | Go-live confirmation, support resources | All channels |
| **Stabilization** | Weeks 1-4 post-launch | Quick wins, common issues and fixes, recognition | Email, champion channels, team meetings |
| **Reinforcement** | Month 2+ | Adoption metrics, success stories, continuous improvement | Newsletters, dashboards, quarterly reviews |

### Feedback Loop Design

Two-way communication is mandatory for effective OCM:

| Mechanism | Purpose | Frequency | Who Reviews |
|-----------|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Pulse surveys (3-5 questions) | Sentiment tracking, concern surface | Bi-weekly during change | OCM Lead |
| Manager check-ins | Local resistance signals | Weekly | Dept Managers + OCM Lead |
| Champion feedback | Ground-level adoption signals | Weekly | OCM Lead |
| Help desk ticket analysis | Friction points in system/process | Weekly | IT + OCM Lead |
| Open Q&A sessions | Surface unvoiced concerns | Monthly | OCM Lead + Subject Experts |

## 5. Training Program Design

### Training Needs Assessment

Before designing training, answer these questions:

1. Who needs training? (role-based segmentation)
2. What must they be able to DO after training? (performance outcomes)
3. What do they already know? (knowledge baseline)
4. What gaps exist? (delta between baseline and target)
5. What is the right delivery method for this audience and content?
6. What are the constraints? (timezone, language, scheduling, technology access)

### Learning Objectives Using Bloom's Taxonomy

Define objectives at the right cognitive level for the task:

| Level | Verb Examples | Use For |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Remember** | Define, list, recall | Terminology, policy facts, system navigation |
| **Understand** | Explain, describe, summarize | Data concepts, process rationale, governance principles |
| **Apply** | Use, execute, demonstrate | Run a report, create a data asset, complete a workflow |
| **Analyze** | Compare, differentiate, examine | Evaluate data quality issues, identify lineage gaps |
| **Evaluate** | Assess, judge, prioritize | Data stewardship decisions, governance policy interpretation |
| **Create** | Design, build, develop | Create governance workflows, design quality rules |

**Example:** "By end of training, the data steward will be able to **create** a new business term with all required attributes in Collibra within 5 minutes without assistance." (Create level)

### Delivery Method Selection

| Method | Best For | Advantages | Limitations |
|--------|----------|------------|-------------|
| Instructor-Led (ILT) | Complex skills, high stakes, relationship-building | Rich interaction, immediate Q&A | Scheduling, scale, cost |
| Virtual ILT (vILT) | Remote/distributed audiences | Flexible geography, recorded | Engagement risk, tech barriers |
| eLearning (async) | Conceptual knowledge, compliance, broad reach | Self-paced, scalable, consistent | Low engagement, no Q&A |
| Job aids / quick reference | Point-of-need support | Immediate application | Not for complex topics |
| Hands-on lab / sandbox | System-specific tasks | Practice without risk | Setup cost, maintenance |
| Peer coaching / shadowing | Applied skills with role models | Contextual, motivational | Scales poorly |
| Video (micro-learning, <5 min) | Process walkthroughs, tips | High engagement, searchable | Not for deep learning |
| Communities of practice | Sustained capability building | Organic, peer learning | Hard to manage quality |

### Train-the-Trainer Model

For large populations, train internal champions to deliver training:

| Step | Activity | Who | Timing |
|------|----------|-----|--------|
| 1. Identify trainers | Select credible, engaged champions from each team | OCM Lead | 8 weeks pre-launch |
| 2. Trainer certification | Train trainers on content + facilitation skills | External/OCM | 4-6 weeks pre-launch |
| 3. Pilot delivery | Trainers deliver to small group with coach observation | Trainers + Coach | 2-3 weeks pre-launch |
| 4. Feedback and calibration | Debrief, adjust materials, confirm trainer readiness | Coach + Trainers | 2 weeks pre-launch |
| 5. Full deployment | Trainers deliver to all target learners | Trainers | Launch window |
| 6. Ongoing support | Trainers remain as coaches post-launch | Trainers | Ongoing |

**Trainer selection criteria:** Subject matter knowledge, credibility with peers, communication skills, willingness.

### Multi-Timezone Scheduling

| Strategy | Application |
|----------|-------------|
| Record all ILT/vILT sessions | Async option for late/early timezones |
| Offer sessions across 3 time bands | Cover Americas, EMEA, APAC |
| Use regional champions for local delivery | Native language, local context |
| Async eLearning as primary path for global roles | Consistent baseline, self-paced |
| Schedule live sessions at overlap hours (e.g., 9am EST / 2pm GMT) | Maximize overlap for interactive sessions |

### Knowledge Assessment Framework

| Level | Format | Passing Threshold | Consequence |
|-------|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| Level 1: Satisfaction | Post-training survey (5-question) | N/A (diagnostic only) | Inform content improvement |
| Level 2: Knowledge | Quiz (10-15 questions) | 80% pass | Must retake if below threshold |
| Level 3: Application | Practical exercise in sandbox | Complete task without error | Certified to access production |
| Level 4: Results | Business metrics (post-launch) | Per adoption targets | Inform reinforcement interventions |

## 6. Data Literacy Programs

### Data Literacy Maturity Levels

| Level | Description | Characteristics |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **1 - Novice** | Little to no data skills | Relies on others, avoids data tasks |
| **2 - Emerging** | Basic awareness | Can read simple reports, asks good questions |
| **3 - Developing** | Functional skills | Uses data tools, builds basic analyses |
| **4 - Proficient** | Independent capability | Interprets complex data, creates insights |
| **5 - Expert** | Advanced/leadership | Mentors others, drives data strategy |

**Baseline assessment** before program design: Survey target population to identify current distribution across levels.

#### Business Analyst / Power User

| Module | Content | Duration | Delivery |

## 7. Resistance Management

### Root Cause Table

Understanding root cause determines the right intervention.

| Root Cause | Description | Indicators |
|------------|-------------|------------|
| **Lack of awareness** | Don't know why change is happening | "Why are we doing this?" "No one told me about this." |
| **Lack of desire** | Know about change, choose not to support | "We tried this before." "What's wrong with how we do it now?" |
| **Fear of job loss** | Believe change threatens their role | "Will I still have a job?" "Can a machine do this now?" |
| **Lack of knowledge** | Don't know how to change | "I don't know how to use the new system." "Training wasn't clear." |
| **Lack of ability** | Tried but can't execute new behavior | High error rates, workarounds, reverting to old ways |
| **Loss of power/control** | Change reduces status, authority, or autonomy | "This takes decisions away from us." "My team loses visibility." |
| **Past change failures** | Previous change efforts failed; credibility gap | "Remember the last time we tried this?" "Management changes priorities every year." |
| **Process or design flaw** | Resistance reflects a legitimate problem | "The new process actually takes longer." "The system doesn't handle our edge cases." |

### Identification Techniques

| Technique | What It Surfaces | When to Use |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| Stakeholder interviews | Deep concerns, political dynamics | During change impact assessment |
| Pulse surveys | Broad sentiment trends | Bi-weekly during change |
| Manager one-on-ones | Team-level resistance, informal leaders | Monthly |
| Champion feedback | Ground-level adoption barriers | Weekly post-launch |
| Help desk analysis | System/process friction (ability resistance) | Weekly post-launch |
| Observation (go-live shadowing) | Actual vs intended behavior | First two weeks post-launch |

### Mitigation Strategies by Root Cause

| Root Cause | Mitigation Approach |
|------------|---------------------|
| Lack of awareness | Targeted communication; increase Why/WIIFM messaging; manager cascade |
| Lack of desire | Sponsor visible engagement; peer success stories; address specific objections; incentives |
| Fear of job loss | Honest conversation about role changes; reframe as skill building; job security commitments from leadership |
| Lack of knowledge | Additional training; job aids; peer coaching; office hours |
| Lack of ability | Practice time; sandbox access; reduced performance expectations during transition; individual coaching |
| Loss of power/control | Involve in design; role clarity documentation; co-creation of new workflows |
| Past change failures | Acknowledge history; demonstrate differences; early wins; credible sponsor acknowledgment |
| Process/design flaw | Escalate to project team; investigate legitimacy; fix the design problem; acknowledge feedback |

### Escalation Path

| Resistance Level | Description | Response |
|------------------|-------------|---------|
| Level 1 - Individual | Single person resisting | Manager conversation; OCM support |
| Level 2 - Team | Small group resisting | Manager intervention + OCM coaching; targeted communication |
| Level 3 - Department | Whole dept resisting | Senior sponsor engagement; OCM investigation of root cause; possible scope/timeline adjustment |
| Level 4 - Org-wide | Broad resistance across organization | Executive intervention; re-examine change design; possible pause and regroup |

### Push-Through vs Accommodate Decision

| Situation | Push Through | Accommodate |
|-----------|:---:|:---:|
| Resistance based on misinformation | Yes | |
| Resistance based on fear with no factual basis | Yes (with empathy) | |
| Resistance reflects a legitimate design problem | | Yes - fix the design |
| Resistance from high-power stakeholder with valid concern | | Yes - co-design solution |
| Timeline cannot move and resistance is preference-based | Yes | |
| Safety, compliance, or regulatory requirement | Yes | Never |
| Resistance signals change saturation | | Yes - adjust sequencing |

## 8. Readiness Assessment

### Five Dimensions of Organizational Readiness

| Dimension | Definition | Assessment Questions |
|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| **Leadership Readiness** | Sponsors are active, visible, and aligned | Are leaders briefed? Do they have talking points? Are they willing to model the change? |
| **Cultural Readiness** | Org culture supports the type of change | Is this org change-tolerant or change-averse? Does change history predict success or failure? |
| **Capacity Readiness** | People have bandwidth to absorb the change | Are key groups overloaded with competing initiatives? Is support staff capacity adequate? |
| **Knowledge Readiness** | People understand what is changing and how | Has awareness campaign reached target groups? Have training requirements been met? |
| **Motivation Readiness** | People have desire to adopt the change | Are stakeholders supportive? Is resistance at manageable levels? |

### Readiness Survey Structure

Administer 2-3 weeks pre-launch. Keep to 10-15 questions max.

**Sample questions (5-point Likert: Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree):**

1. I understand why this change is happening.
2. I understand what will be different about my job after this change.
3. I have had adequate time to prepare for this change.
4. My manager has explained what this change means for our team.
5. I feel confident using the new [system/process] after training.
6. I believe this change will benefit [me / my team / the organization].
7. I know where to go if I have questions or problems after go-live.

**Open-ended questions:**
- What concerns do you still have about this change?
- What additional support would help you be successful?

### Go/No-Go Criteria

| Dimension | Go Threshold | No-Go Trigger | Mitigation if No-Go |
|-----------|:---:|:---:|-----|
| Leadership Readiness | All key sponsors briefed; >80% say they are ready to communicate | Any sponsor not briefed | Sponsor enablement session; delay launch 1 week |
| Communication Reach | >80% of target audience received key messages | <60% reached | Emergency communication blast; manager cascade |
| Training Completion | >90% of required roles trained | <75% completion | Additional sessions; role-based go-live sequencing |
| Knowledge Assessment | >80% passing rate in assessments | <70% passing | Remediation training; targeted coaching |
| System Readiness | User acceptance testing passed; no P1 defects open | Open P1 defects | Technical fix before launch |
| Resistance Level | No Level 3/4 resistance active | Active Level 3/4 resistance | Escalate to sponsor; address or accept risk |

### Readiness Remediation Plan Template

For each gap identified, document:

| Gap | Dimension | Severity | Remediation Action | Owner | Target Date |
|----|-----------|:---:|----|----|---|
| 40% of analysts not trained | Knowledge | High | Two additional sessions + recordings | Training Lead | 3 days pre-launch |
| Dept A manager not briefed | Leadership | Critical | 1:1 with sponsor before launch | OCM Lead | 5 days pre-launch |
| High resistance in Finance | Motivation | High | Finance-specific WIIFM session with CFO | Sponsor + OCM | 1 week pre-launch |

## 9. Adoption Metrics

### Metric Categories

| Category | Definition | Example Metrics |
|----------|------------|----------------|
| **Usage** | Are people using the system/process? | Logins per week, features accessed, transactions processed |
| **Proficiency** | Are people using it correctly? | Error rate, assessment scores, support ticket rate |
| **Satisfaction** | Do people feel good about the change? | NPS, satisfaction survey score, voluntary usage rate |
| **Outcome** | Is the change delivering business value? | Cost reduction, cycle time, data quality score improvement |

### Leading vs Lagging Indicators

| Indicator Type | Examples | When Available | Use For |
|----------------|----------|:---:|---------|
| **Leading** | Training completion, communication reach, readiness scores, champion activity | Before go-live + early post-launch | Predict adoption risk; intervene early |
| **Lagging** | Process compliance rate, error rate, ROI realization, productivity improvement | 30-90 days post-launch | Confirm adoption success; report to stakeholders |

**Rule:** Don't wait for lagging indicators to confirm failure. Leading indicators give you time to intervene.

### Adoption Metric Dashboard Example

| Metric | Target | Week 1 | Week 2 | Week 4 | Status |
|--------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| System logins (% of users) | 80% | 45% | 62% | 79% | On track |
| Training completion | 90% | 88% | 92% | 94% | Met |
| Support tickets (weekly) | <20 | 47 | 31 | 18 | Improving |
| User satisfaction (1-5) | >3.5 | 3.1 | 3.4 | 3.7 | On track |
| Process compliance (audit) | 90% | N/A | N/A | 72% | At risk |

### Kirkpatrick's 4 Levels of Evaluation

| Level | What It Measures | How to Measure | When |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|------|
| **Level 1: Reaction** | Did learners like the training? | Post-training satisfaction survey | Immediately after training |
| **Level 2: Learning** | Did learners gain knowledge/skill? | Pre/post quiz, practical assessment | Immediately after training |
| **Level 3: Behavior** | Are learners applying learning on the job? | Manager observation, system usage data, audit | 30-60 days post-launch |
| **Level 4: Results** | Did the change deliver business outcomes? | Business metrics (productivity, quality, cost) | 90+ days post-launch |

**Minimum standard:** Measure all four levels for significant changes. Level 1-2 for minor changes.

## 10. Sustainability and Reinforcement

### Post-Go-Live Reinforcement Cadence

| Timeframe | Activities | Purpose |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Week 1-2** | Daily champion check-ins; help desk escalation review; manager pulse | Catch critical adoption failures early |
| **Week 3-4** | Weekly adoption dashboard review; targeted coaching for laggards; quick win celebration | Stabilize adoption |
| **Month 2-3** | Bi-weekly adoption review; address persistent resistance; advanced training for power users | Deepen proficiency |
| **Month 4-6** | Monthly review; transition to BAU support; evaluate sustainability metrics | Normalize new behaviors |
| **Month 6+** | Quarterly review; annual refresher training; ongoing improvement | Sustain and improve |

### Change Champion Network

**Purpose:** Peer-to-peer support and influence is more effective than top-down communication alone.

**Champion profile:**
- Credible with peers (not necessarily senior)
- Willing and enthusiastic
- Representative of target stakeholder groups
- Accessible to their peer group

**Champion activities:**
- Frontline Q&A for their team
- Provide feedback to OCM Lead (ground-level signal)
- Model the change in daily work
- Facilitate peer learning and support
- Recognize and celebrate team adoption wins

**Champion enablement:**
- Pre-launch: Deep training on system/process + change management basics
- Launch: Clear role description, talking points, escalation path
- Post-launch: Weekly champion calls; recognition program; direct access to project team

**Champion-to-user ratio:** Target 1 champion per 10-15 end users for high-complexity changes.

### Ongoing Training Program

| Program Element | Description | Frequency |
|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| New joiner onboarding | Role-based training for new hires | As needed (within 30 days of join) |
| Refresher training | Annual update on changes, policy updates | Annual |
| Advanced skills | Deeper training for champions and power users | As needed |
| Just-in-time resources | Intranet library of videos, job aids, FAQs | Updated continuously |
| Community of practice | Monthly peer learning sessions | Monthly |

### Continuous Improvement Process

```
Measure adoption metrics
        ↓
Identify gaps and underperformance
        ↓
Root cause analysis (is it awareness, knowledge, ability, design?)
        ↓
Targeted intervention (communication, training, coaching, design fix)
        ↓
Measure improvement
        ↓
Repeat
```

### Governance Integration

Embed the change in organizational governance to prevent backsliding:

| Governance Element | Action |
|-------------------|--------|
| Role definitions | Update job descriptions to include new responsibilities |
| Performance management | Add data governance behaviors to performance criteria |
| Policy documentation | Update SOPs, process guides, and policy documents |
| New employee onboarding | Embed in standard onboarding program |
| Manager expectations | Include change adoption in manager objectives |
| Audit and compliance | Add adoption compliance to internal audit program |

## 11. Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|-------------|--------------|-----|
| **"Big bang" communication** | One town hall is not enough; people need multiple touchpoints | Plan multi-channel, multi-touchpoint communication over full change lifecycle |
| **Training too close to go-live** | Learners forget by launch day; anxiety peaks | Start awareness 8+ weeks out; deliver skills training 2-4 weeks pre-launch |
| **One-size-fits-all training** | Executives don't need what end users need; waste and irrelevance erode engagement | Design role-based training tracks with different depth and content |
| **No resistance plan** | Resistance surprises teams that don't plan for it | Proactively identify potential resistance during impact assessment; pre-build mitigation plans |
| **No sponsor engagement** | "Do as I say" doesn't work; people watch what leaders do | Define active and visible sponsor behaviors; coach sponsors; hold them accountable |
| **Measuring only satisfaction** | Smile sheets ≠ adoption | Measure behavior change (Level 3) and business results (Level 4), not just reactions |
| **Launching into readiness gaps** | Gaps identified but ignored; launch fails | Enforce go/no-go criteria; delay if critical thresholds not met |
| **OCM ends at go-live** | Adoption often peaks at go-live and then drops without reinforcement | Plan explicit reinforcement for 6 months post-launch |
| **No feedback loops** | OCM team flies blind; problems fester | Build structured feedback mechanisms (surveys, champion calls, manager check-ins) |
| **Champions with no support** | Nominated champions without enablement quickly burn out | Invest in champion training; provide regular briefings and recognition |
